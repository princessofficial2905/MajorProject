Overview

Abstract

This research focuses on the development and analysis of a hybrid composite material composed of banana fibers, carbon fibers, nano zinc oxide (ZnO) fillers, and an epoxy matrix. With the growing demand for sustainable, lightweight, and high-performance materials, this study integrates natural and synthetic fibers with advanced nano-fillers to enhance the composite's mechanical properties.

The investigation primarily aims to determine the elastic properties of the composite using analytical approaches, specifically the Chamis and Modified Halpin-Tsai models. These methods predict key parameters including longitudinal modulus, transverse modulus, shear modulus, and Poisson's ratio, which provide insights into the composite's stiffness, flexibility, and load-bearing capabilities.

The study systematically varied the composition of banana fibers, carbon fibers, and ZnO fillers to identify the optimal combination for superior mechanical performance. The results showed that the composite with 3% ZnO exhibited the best mechanical properties, demonstrating an ideal balance between strength and flexibility. This hybrid composite demonstrates great potential for use in structural and industrial applications, including automotive, aerospace, and marine industries.

Chapter One

Introduction

Background & Demand for Sustainability

The growing global concern for sustainability has fuelled a huge interest in sustainable production of materials, with this new development in natural fibre-reinforced composites. The growing trend is attributed to a necessity to minimize the use of synthetic non-biodegradable material and lower environmental impacts. Natural fibres, which are made from renewable sources, offer a highly plausible alternative — they are light, affordable, and biodegradable.

Growing environmental issues and depleting fossil fuels have pushed the need for sustainable materials. Natural fibre composites are perfectly in sync with the circular economy model, where they use recyclable material and minimize wastage. The shift towards sustainability is not just a green imperative but also an economic one, generating innovations and new markets for bio-based products.

Natural vs Synthetic Fibres

Natural fibres of plant, animal, or mineral origin are increasingly being utilized as reinforcement in composites. Out of all natural fibres, banana fibres have found great interest as they are rich in composition, cost-effective, and possess good mechanical properties. Extracted from banana pseudo-stems, they represent significant agricultural waste recovery.

While synthetic fibres like carbon fibres possess better mechanical properties — high strength and stiffness — natural fibres enjoy sustainability advantages. To leverage both, researchers have developed hybrid composites, combining various types of fibres into a single matrix to create synergistic optimal performance.

Why Nano-ZnO?

Nano zinc oxide (ZnO) is a functionally versatile metal oxide that makes it highly desirable to introduce into composite materials:

🔥 Thermal Stability

Nano ZnO increases thermal stability, critical for components exposed to high-temperature environments like engine covers and heat shields.

⚙️ Mechanical Properties

Addition of nano ZnO results in remarkable improvement in tensile strength, stiffness, and hardness through improved load transfer and interfacial adhesion.

🛡️ Antimicrobial

ZnO possesses strong antimicrobial properties, critical for medical, packaging, and textile applications requiring hygiene and microbial resistance.

🔬 Nano-Scale Benefit

Nano-particles possess high surface area-to-volume ratio, enabling good dispersion, higher interfacial area, and more interaction with the polymer matrix and fibres.

Applications

🚗
Automotive Industry

Door panels, dashboards, seat frames, outer body panels — banana-carbon hybrid composites reduce weight while improving fuel efficiency. Nano ZnO thermal stability is crucial for engine covers and heat shields. Especially impactful for electric vehicles where weight reduction directly extends driving range.

🏥
Medical Field

Biodegradable natural fibre composites for short-term implants and drug delivery. Carbon fibre reinforced composites for orthopaedic implants, bone plates, screws, and joint replacements. ZnO's antibacterial properties reduce infection risk in surgical instruments and wound dressings.

🏗️
Construction

Non-structural products like partition walls and insulating panels. Structural members — beams, bridges, columns — where high stiffness is required. ZnO improves fire resistance and inhibits mould growth, enhancing indoor air quality. Prefabricated structures benefit from the lightness and easy installation.

Literature Review

Natural fibre composites (NFCs) have been a major research focus for decades due to their presence as eco-friendly substitutes to synthetic fibre composites. NFCs are biologically renewable materials offering distinctive combinations of environmental advantages and attractive structural attributes.

Although NFCs carry several benefits, key challenges include their hydrophilic nature causing water absorption, dimensional instability, poor interfacial adhesion with hydrophobic polymer matrices, and inhomogeneity in fibre properties across species and cultivation conditions.

Research Gaps

Despite significant progress, key gaps remain. The most apparent gap is in the efficient use of hybrid composites where different fibre types are combined for synergistic property improvements. Specifically, blending banana fibres with high-strength carbon fibres as a hybrid composite structure requires thorough study. Additionally, optimal concentration, dispersion, and interaction mechanisms of nano-fillers within composite matrices remain undetermined.

Research Objectives

  • Optimal proportion identification — Identify the optimal mixture of banana fibres, carbon fibres, and nano ZnO in an epoxy matrix for optimal combination of mechanical properties, stiffness, and functional attributes.
  • Chamis analytical model analysis — Use the Chamis micromechanical model to calculate elastic properties for different composite compositions and compare estimated values of elastic modulus, Poisson's ratio, and shear modulus.
  • Modified Halpin-Tsai model analysis — Utilize the Modified Halpin-Tsai model for estimation of elastic properties and compare with Chamis model results to establish precision and limitations of both models.
  • Validation against literature — Verify results obtained from analytical models by comparing them with experimental results and literature to check validity and credibility of the models.

3.1 Chamis Model

The Chamis model is a well-known micromechanical model to estimate elastic properties of composites reinforced with fibres. It assumes perfect matrix-fibre adhesion, isotropic fibre orientation, and uniform filler distribution — providing stiffness and load-carrying capacity estimates.

Vf = (mf/ρf) / (mf/ρf + mm/ρm) [Fiber Volume Fraction]
Vm = 1 - Vf [Matrix Volume Fraction]

E₁₁ = Vfb · E₁₁fb + Vmx · Emx [Longitudinal Elastic Modulus]
E₂₂ = Emx / (1 − √Vfb) [Transverse Elastic Modulus]
G₁₂ = Gmx / (1 − √Vfb) [In-Plane Shear Modulus]
G₂₃ = Gmx / [1 − √Vfb(1 − Gmx/G₂₃fb)] [Transverse Shear Modulus]
ν₁₂ = Vfb · ν₁₂fb + Vmx · νmx [In-Plane Poisson's Ratio]
ν₂₃ = (E₂₂ / E₁₁) × ν₁₂ [Transverse Poisson's Ratio]

3.2 Modified Halpin-Tsai Model

The Modified Halpin-Tsai model incorporates the effects of filler geometry, size, and volume fraction on the composite's stiffness. It is particularly effective for composites with nano-fillers, incorporating filler-matrix interaction to improve mechanical property prediction accuracy.

E₂ = Emx · (1 + ξₑ₂ · ηₑ₂ · Vcf) / (1 − ξₑ₂ · Vcf)
G₁₂ = Gmx · (1 + ξg₁₂ · ηg₁₂ · Vcf) / (1 − ηg₁₂ · Vcf)

Where Vcf < 0.3:
ηₑ₂ = (E₂cf / Emx) − 1
ξₑ₂ = 4.924 − 35.888·Vcf + 125.118·Vcf² − 145.121·Vcf³
ξg₁₂ = 1 + 40·Vcf¹⁰

E₁ = E₁cf·Vcf + E₁bf·Vbf + Emx·Vmx + Enc·Vnc + Eip·Vip
ν₁₂ = ν₁₂cf·Vcf + ν₁₂bf·Vbf + νmx·Vmx + νnc·Vnc + νip·Vip

4.4 Model Comparison & Error Analysis

Results indicated that the FE model gave E₁ values of 18,217 MPa, 18,884 MPa, and 19,547 MPa for 1%, 2%, and 3% ZnO composites, respectively. The Halpin-Tsai model predicted the highest longitudinal modulus with extremely high predicted values, whereas the Chamis model was less accurate for transverse modulus and shear modulus.

For transverse modulus (E₂), the FE model gave 11,376 MPa, 11,764 MPa, and 11,367 MPa. Chamis model predictions were very high (38,300 MPa, 40,100 MPa, and 41,700 MPa) with large deviations. The Halpin-Tsai model, though improved over Chamis, still reported discrepancies with FEA results.

The Halpin-Tsai model showed the highest average error in estimation of ν₁₂ (Poisson's ratio), overestimating it significantly — values of 1.04, 0.26, and 0.28 against FEA values of 0.11, 0.11, and 0.12, respectively.

4.5 Experimental Testing Methodology

ZnO composite samples were fabricated using the hand lay-up technique and underwent post-curing at room temperature for 24 hours, followed by 80°C for 3 hours for maximum cross-linking. Physical testing per ASTM standards:

Tensile Testing · ASTM D3039

Universal testing machine at 2 mm/min crosshead speed. Results: 1% ZnO → 45 MPa · 2% ZnO → 52 MPa · 3% ZnO → 48 MPa. Optimal at 2% ZnO.

Flexural Testing · ASTM D790

Three-point bend test. Flexural strength highest at 2% ZnO, decreasing slightly at 3% due to nanoparticle agglomeration and non-uniform stress distribution.

Impact Test · ASTM D256

2% ZnO composite exhibited the highest impact resistance, stiffness, and toughness. Validated ZnO reinforcement's impact on energy absorption capacity.

SEM Analysis

Scanning Electron Microscopy on fractured samples revealed ZnO agglomerates at 3% loading, confirming nanoparticle agglomeration decreased fibre-matrix adhesion.

Chapter Five

Conclusions

The composite with 2% ZnO exhibited minimum deformation (0.284339 mm) and maximum structural stiffness, while 3% ZnO showed the best analytical mechanical properties — demonstrating an ideal balance between strength and flexibility for engineering applications.

Preparation and characterization of a new hybrid composite material of banana fibres, carbon fibres, nano-zinc oxide, and epoxy resin has been very promising for engineering applications. The introduction of nano ZnO enhances composite mechanical properties through increased stiffness, tensile strength, and toughness. Thermal stability is also increased with ZnO nanoparticles, qualifying the material for high-temperature applications. The ratio of banana fibres to carbon fibres in the composite provides balance between stiffness and sustainability.

Analytical models (Chamis and Modified Halpin-Tsai) were compared to FEA analyses built on ANSYS software. Validation confirmed that the Halpin-Tsai model gave more accurate composite elastic predictions, while the Chamis model showed larger discrepancies for transverse moduli and shear moduli values. FEA predictions showed excellent agreement with test results, supporting the utility of computational solutions in predicting composite behaviour.

Key Findings

  • Nano-fillers improve mechanical properties of composites up to an optimal percentage — beyond which agglomeration reduces effectiveness
  • Hybrid composites with banana and carbon fibers show enhanced tensile strength and flexibility over single-fiber composites
  • Analytical models like Chamis and Halpin-Tsai effectively predict elastic properties but show varying accuracy for different parameters
  • The Halpin-Tsai model is more accurate for longitudinal elastic modulus (E₁), while FEA provides the most reliable overall prediction
  • 2% ZnO is the optimum reinforcement ratio for maximum strength, hardness, and minimum deformation (0.284339 mm)
  • The composite demonstrates broad applicability in automotive, biomedical, and construction sectors

Future Scope

Nano ZnO content optimization remains a top priority, as higher content causes particle agglomeration with adverse effects. Research should continue to promote advanced dispersion techniques for nano-fillers to maximize reinforcing ability. Surface treatment procedures can develop interfacial adhesion between natural fibers and epoxy matrix for enhanced load transfer and longevity. Life cycle studies must be conducted to assess the environmental acceptability of NFCs comprehensively.
